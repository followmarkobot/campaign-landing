Battles are won by the side with the best equipment.

Obama won in 2008 by being first to Facebook ads. Trump won in 2016 by being first to Twitter.

2026 will be won by whoever figures out AI first.

Not AI that writes generic slop—but campaigns that trained it to sound like their candidate, know their voters, and run while they sleep.

Are you doing all of these?

■ Recording everything

Every meeting, every event, every call. Your campaign is generating insights constantly. Most of it disappears.

■ Transcribing automatically

Tools like Otter or Granola join your meetings and transcribe them for you. No more "can someone take notes?"

■ Storing transcripts in one folder

This becomes your campaign brain. Every decision, every talking point, every policy position—searchable and ready to feed to AI.

■ Turning long videos into social clips

Vizard takes a 30-minute town hall and cuts it into 10 short clips for social media. What used to take an intern a full day takes 10 minutes.

■ Assigning one person to own AI

Doesn't need to be an expert. A college volunteer who's curious and gets 5 hours a week to experiment. That's enough to stay ahead.

■ Training your team on the tools

The tools are useless if your team is afraid of them. One workshop changes everything.

Everyone has the same 24 hours. Most campaigns use them the same way. But in every race, one campaign uses them differently—and it won't be clear who until November.

If this list feels overwhelming, start with #1 and #2. Record and transcribe. Everything else builds from there.
